My Faces of Innovation (Before and During MSV)

The Hurdler: Math was one of my favorite subjects growing up. I enjoyed how a math problem could have multiple methods to solving for the correct answer. Unlimited perspectives to a singular problem. Post-Graduation (UC Berkeley Class of 2020), I was passionate to recreate the concept of ‘work’ by creating an HRMS Product. My cofounder and I had a personal budget of $50,000 to create an MVP product in 8 months that could disrupt traditional recruiting and people management. Innovating under constraints was challenging but the lack of resources (time, money, and skills) only motivated us to work smarter and the added risk of being jobless only excited us. But, as the 8 months started to expire, we were faced with a key hurdle: Investment. Unfortunately, after weeks of networking and pitching our HRMS product, we were staring down the same wall of an empty bank account. I was too stubborn to move-on and wanted to maintain a quiet and positive determination to impact the community of job seekers. As a hurdler, I was able to turn this obstacle into an opportunity. If we could get invested for the product, was it possible to get invested for the people? I wanted to create an impact on my community as an entrepreneur, but I did not have to do it with the HRMS product. I then decided to join one of the funds (MSV) I had pitched to as a Growth Analyst in a full-time role. I was able to solve the problem of Investment, by pivoting my perspective on what needed to be funded. Growing my experience and skills as an entrepreneur was more important and the right answer while the success of the product was not necessarily needed to create that impact (at that point of time). My Hurdler persona allowed me to pivot my resources (time, money, and skills) to a larger platform (the fund) to impact the communities I cared about through entrepreneurship.

The Cross Pollinator: Traveling and living in a new city once every three years of life gave me the ability to learn quickly (adaptability and curiosity) and empathize with different types of people around the world. MSV was led by 10 executives, all with niche areas of expertise, with me as the sole analyst. Since I reported to these executives, who were respective leaders of their functions (CFO, CTO, General Counsel, etc.), I acted as the glue that could translate and drive for success across these siloed functional areas. As an entrepreneur with experience in developing products, I was able to provide interesting input for each of the executives on project work, whether it was creating draft legal contracts for my general counsel or creating commercial models for my COO. Moreover, since we were a technology fund, I believe my wide interests in technology (Datascience, Cloud, AI/ML, and other Technology Infrastructure products and services) and an aptitude for teaching also benefited the fund as we explored startups to invest in, mentored product founders, or incubated new products/services. Specifically, as a part of MSV, I was the lead Product Manager for a Reg-tech Startup. Due to my unique background, we were able to borrow principles of cybersecurity program management into regulation technology (Policy Management, Controls, Regulations, etc.). My Cross-Pollinator persona helped me break traditional operational and technical siloes to provide value to the fund horizontally.

Improvements to my Faces of Innovation

Although I believe I carry the personas of the Hurdler and the Cross Pollinator, there needs to be improvements made in how I learn and build. Empathy (through desirability) must be baked into the learning and building processes to bring business benefits to an organization. I don’t intend to be the best at each of these personas, but I would like to achieve true excellence in a few areas and strength in many. I would like to focus on strengthening my Anthropologist and the Experience Architect personas by improving my skills in the following areas:

  1. Reduce bias and practice the principle of the “beginner’s mind” by being more open minded to new ideas, especially during Discovery or Ideation Sprints at my workplace.
  2. Observe, don’t judge and find intrinsic rewards to learning about/from other people. Unfortunately, extrinsic motivation can only get you so far in carrying a good Anthropologist persona.
  3. Use my skills as a Cross Pollinator and Hurdler to better listen to my intuition in drawing insights about human behavior. The ten faces have complementary skills that could be used interchangeably when making improvements.
  4. Notice new or broken things (looking beyond what is visible, even in ‘trash cans’) in daily lives to sharpen observation skills.
  5. Draw on all available experiences of my lifetime from a locker of experiences (merit badging) and constantly add to them. I want to be a constant learner, and new experiences unlocks observations on what I see and why.
  6. Innovate on Experiences (and the respective journeys of the consumer) beyond traditional product systems by focusing on trigger points. As a technology fund, we would primarily compete on products, but by improving my skills as an Experience Architect, I want to be able to empathize and provide a more rewarding experience to consumers.
  7. Promote Authenticity (and what is real, honest, and meaningful) in experiences as it related to the organization, products/services, the customers, digital interactions, events, and my internal teams.
Moving Forward

Moving forward, I plan on using a combination of the Hurdler, Cross Pollinator, The Anthropologist and The Experience Architect faces across learning, organizing, and building, as a Product Manager. Practicing and Improving on the above skills would be crucial in adding empathy to my operational and technical breadth (discussed above) to become a more holistic T-Shaped Person. I plan on:

  • Adding guest members (of a different face of Innovation) to daily scrum calls or discovery sessions to source alternative methods of solving key problems (cross-pollination or collaboration).
  • Adding powerful internal tools (with the recommendations of a Set Designer) in our armory to impact the behavior and attitude of people and the speed and convenience of innovation in our organization.
  • Focus on Empathy within teams, events, organizations to design products/services that meet human needs by understanding human behavior and attitudes (A focus on qualitative research, in conjunction to quantitative).
  • Bring more observations and insights of my experiences into key meetings while continuously creating new experiences through my personal hobbies (Dirt Bikes, Traveling, and Food)
  • Seek out and pitch diverse projects (under the mission of MSV) that could be cross- pollinated to my boss. For example: Applying the principles of a cybersecurity program to AI/ML Governance.
  • Faces and Organization

I believe an organization needs to hire for a good mix of faces or personas to be truly innovative and successful. Combining several personas together and blending them into a multi- disciplinary team creates complementary effects in generating a positive force for innovation. As a recruiter, I would hire individuals based on where they fit in the 10 different personas so that the team or organization could add up to a healthy score and mix of faces. As stated in the Ten Faces of Innovation, this would allow you to create a team that outperforms competition in the full range of daily tests that every company faces. Mixing such different personalities allows for creative abrasion and organizational progress at the people (or HR) level.

You could recognize these personas in a traditional organizational structure by tagging your workforce based on the personas they exhibit strengths in. You could change the composition of teams (depending on the project) based on these personas to have them be complementary. For example, you might want a software engineer who is an anthropologist, a product manager who is a collaborator, a designer who is a storyteller, and a founder that is a hurdler to start creating a new product/service. You do not need to get rid of traditional roles already existing in the organization that define function (which possesses roots in how the military is managed). Moreover, in such an organization, I would look for bosses or mentors who nurture a complementary set of personas that could benefit my professional growth. Personally, I would look for an anthropologist or an experience architect type boss or mentor (regardless of traditional roles) who I could trust, not only in creating value to the organization, but value in myself, in the context of innovation.
